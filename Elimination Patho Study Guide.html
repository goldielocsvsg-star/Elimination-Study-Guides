What is Elimination?

Elimination is the process of excreting waste from the body

Urinary Elimination (Micturition/Voiding): Passing urine out of the urinary tract

üìöKey Terminology

Term Definition
Continence Purposeful, VOLUNTARY control over elimination
Incontinence LOSS of control - involuntary leakage of urine or feces
Retention Keeping materials inside that should be expelled
Micturition The act of urinating/voiding

üö®Types of Urinary Incontinence

Type Description Trigger/Cause
Stress Small urine leakage with increased abdominal pressure Coughing, sneezing, laughing, exercising, jumping
Urge Large amounts leak at unexpected times (even sleeping) Sudden, strong, uncontrollable need to urinate
Overactive Bladder (OAB) Urinary frequency & urgency, with or without leakage Bladder muscle contractions
Functional Can't get to toilet in time due to physical/cognitive issues Mobility problems, confusion, environmental barriers
Mixed Combination of stress AND urge incontinence Multiple factors
Transient Temporary leakage that will resolve Infection, new medication, severe cough

üî¥Urinary Retention

Definition: Inability to fully empty the bladder (partial or complete)

Type Onset Clinical Findings
Acute Rapid, sudden Pressure/severe pain over pubic area; NO urine output for hours; restlessness, sweating
Chronic Slow, gradual ‚Üì volume voided; straining; frequency/urgency; small voids; may have incontinence

‚ö†Ô∏è Consequences of Urinary Retention

  • Pain and discomfort
  • Chronic bladder infections (UTIs)
  • Bladder distention (overstretching)
  • Urinary reflux ‚Üí urine flows back to ureters
  • Pyelonephritis (kidney infection)
  • Renal atrophy (kidney shrinkage) üö®

ü¶†Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)

Pathophysiology: Bacteria (usually E. coli from bowel) travel UP the urethra and infect the bladder. They multiply and irritate the bladder lining.

Key Facts:

  • The urinary tract is normally STERILE (unlike the bowel)
  • 5th most common healthcare-associated infection (HAI)
  • If UTI develops 72+ hours after admission = likely HAI
  • Most often caused by E. coli

üí° Prevention is KEY!

  • Strict infection control & hygiene
  • Proper perineal care
  • Medical & surgical asepsis
  • Minimize catheter use
  • Use sterile technique for catheterization
  • Prevent CAUTIs (Catheter-Associated UTIs)

üîÑUrinary Diversions

Surgical procedure creating a NEW route for urine to exit, bypassing the bladder

Continent Reservoir

Internal pouch holds urine

Patient empties via small stoma using catheter

Example: Mitrofanoff procedure (uses appendix as passageway to belly button)

Incontinent Diversion

External collection bag

Urine flows continuously from ureters into pouch

Example: Ileoconduit

  • Hold urine for 1-2 hours

  • Weakened pelvic floor muscles
  • Increased risk of incontinence
  • Prostate enlargement (men)

ü©∫Nursing Assessment

  3. I&O: Document Intake & Output; assess urine color, clarity, odor
  4. Labs: Urinalysis, urine culture

Bowel Elimination

Definition: Passing stool (feces) out of the intestinal tract through defecation (bowel movement)

üí©Common Bowel Problems

Problem Pathophysiology (How It Goes Wrong)
Constipation Colon absorbs TOO MUCH water from stool; peristalsis slows down ‚Üí hard, dry, difficult-to-pass stool
Diarrhea Bowel lining secretes too much fluid OR waste moves too fast ‚Üí prevents water absorption
Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) Immune system attacks GI lining ‚Üí chronic inflammation, ulcers, bleeding.
Crohn's: Any GI part. Ulcerative Colitis: Colon only

üò∞Consequences of Fecal Retention

  • Abdominal pain & discomfort
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Stool becomes dry and hard
  • Fecal impaction (stool blocks intestine)
  • Ileus (loss of GI muscle activity - often post-surgery/trauma)

‚ö†Ô∏èRisk Factors for Bowel Problems

  • Age: Children may ignore urge; older adults have ‚Üì fiber/fluid intake & exercise
  • Pregnancy: Progesterone relaxes muscles; uterus pressure ‚Üí constipation
  • Neurological impairment: Nerve damage affects bowel control
  • Altered mobility: Can't reach toilet ‚Üí ignoring urge ‚Üí retention
  • Medications: Opioids, iron, anticholinergics cause constipation
  • Diet: Low fiber, low fluid intake

What Are Diuretics?

Medications that increase urine production to remove excess fluid from the body

"Water Pills" that help kidneys eliminate sodium and water

ü´òKidney Functions (Review)

  1. Cleanse extracellular fluid (ECF)
  2. Maintain acid-base balance
  3. Excrete metabolic waste & foreign substances

Three Basic Renal Processes

  1. Filtration - occurs at the glomerulus
  2. Reabsorption - 99% of water, electrolytes, nutrients returned to blood
  3. Active Tubular Secretion - removes waste into urine

‚öôÔ∏èHow Diuretics Work

Mechanism of Action:

  • Diuretics BLOCK sodium (Na‚Å∫) and chloride (Cl‚Åª) reabsorption at specific sites in the nephron
  • When these electrolytes can't be reabsorbed ‚Üí water stays in the tubule
  • More water in tubule = MORE URINE excreted
  • Greatest diuresis site: Proximal tubule

‚ö†Ô∏èAdverse Effects Common to ALL Diuretics

Because diuretics remove fluid AND electrolytes, they ALL carry these risks:

  • Hypovolemia (low fluid volume) / Dehydration
  • Hypotension (low blood pressure) - due to fluid loss
  • Electrolyte imbalances (‚Üì sodium, ‚Üì chloride, ‚Üì potassium)
  • Acid-base imbalances

üíäTypes of Diuretics

Class Examples Site of Action Key Points
Loop Diuretics Furosemide (Lasix), Bumetanide Loop of Henle MOST POWERFUL! Watch K+ levels. Can cause ototoxicity
Thiazide Diuretics Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ) Distal tubule First-line for HTN. Monitor K+, glucose, uric acid
Potassium-Sparing Spironolactone, Amiloride Collecting duct WEAKEST. KEEPS potassium! Risk: hyperkalemia
Osmotic Diuretics Mannitol Proximal tubule & loop For cerebral edema, increased ICP. IV only

ü©∫Nursing Considerations for Diuretics

Monitor Closely

  • Daily weights (same time, same scale)
  • I&O (Intake & Output)
  • BP (watch for hypotension)
  • Electrolytes (especially K+)
  • Signs of dehydration

Patient Education

  • Take in morning (avoid nocturia)
  • Change positions slowly
  • Report dizziness, weakness
  • Weigh daily at home
  • Know signs of low K+

Diet Considerations

  • Loop/Thiazide: Eat K+-rich foods (bananas, oranges, spinach)
  • K+-sparing: AVOID excess K+ foods
  • Monitor sodium intake

Safety

  • Fall risk ‚Üë (from hypotension)
  • Assess for dizziness
  • Bathroom accessibility
  • Avoid driving if dizzy

Consequence: Inability to remove toxins, metabolic waste, and fluids

Result: Build-up can lead to DEATH

Treatment: Dialysis or kidney transplant

‚ö†Ô∏èRed Flag Alert!

üî¥ Urinary Red Flags

  • No urine output for hours = acute retention
  • Severe suprapubic pain = distended bladder
  • Fever + flank pain = pyelonephritis (kidney infection)
  • Hematuria (blood in urine) = infection, trauma, or tumor

üî¥ Bowel Red Flags

  • No BM for 3+ days with pain = impaction risk
  • Blood in stool = GI bleeding, cancer screening needed
  • Severe cramping + fever = possible IBD flare or infection
  • Absent bowel sounds = ileus (emergency!)

üî¥ Diuretic Red Flags

  • Severe hypotension = hypovolemia
  • Muscle weakness/cramps = electrolyte imbalance
  • Confusion/lethargy = severe dehydration or electrolyte issue
  • Irregular heartbeat = K+ imbalance

‚úÖ NCLEX SUCCESS TIPS FOR ELIMINATION ‚úÖ

  • STERILE TECHNIQUE: Always use with catheterization - CAUTIs are preventable!
  • I&O MATTERS: Monitor intake and output closely, especially with diuretics
  • ELECTROLYTES: Watch potassium levels with ALL diuretics
  • SAFETY FIRST: Incontinence = fall risk! Ensure toilet access
  • CULTURE MATTERS: Psychosocial implications - maintain dignity and privacy
  • PREVENTION: Hydration, fiber, mobility, toileting schedules work!
  • TIMING: Give diuretics in the MORNING to avoid nocturia
